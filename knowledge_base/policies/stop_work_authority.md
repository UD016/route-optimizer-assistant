## Purpose

This policy outlines situations where Power Generation (PG) technicians have the right and responsibility to stop work due to unsafe working conditions.

The safety of employees takes precedence over operational, customer, or scheduling requirements. Work must not continue until identified hazards have been eliminated or adequately controlled.

---

# Stop Work Authority

Every employee has both the **right** and the **responsibility** to stop work whenever they believe the work environment presents an unacceptable safety risk.

Work may only resume once the identified hazards have been eliminated or properly mitigated.

---

# Examples of Stop Work Situations

## Environmental Hazards

Technicians may stop work when environmental conditions create a health or safety risk.

Examples include:

- Presence of a mouse nest.
- Presence of rodent droppings or other biological contamination.

---

## Unsafe Generator Access

Technicians must not access equipment when safe access cannot be ensured.

Examples include:

- Damaged or unsecured access ladder.
- Unstable ground creating a fall hazard.
- Missing, damaged, or unsecured guardrails.
- Roof work without adequate fall protection.
- Cluttered work area creating trip hazards.

---

## Weather Conditions

Work must not proceed when weather conditions prevent safe access.

Examples include:

- Significant ice around the generator.
- Heavy snow preventing safe access.
- Slippery walking surfaces.

The customer is responsible for ensuring safe access to the equipment before work begins.

---

## Customer Requests Unsafe Work

Technicians are not required to continue working when a customer requests that safety procedures be ignored.

Examples include:

- Customer refuses Lock Out / Tag Out (LOTO) because of operational requirements.
- Customer requests work to continue despite known hazards.

Safety procedures must always take precedence over customer requests.

---

## Working Alone

Certain tasks require more than one technician to ensure safe execution.

Examples include:

- Accessing a rooftop through a hatch using a ladder.
- Any work where immediate assistance may be required in the event of:
  - Injury
  - Medical emergency
  - Fall
  - Loss of consciousness

When a second technician is required for safety reasons, work must not proceed until adequate personnel are available.

---

# Coordinator Responsibilities

Before dispatching a technician, coordinators should consider whether the planned work presents known safety risks.

When applicable:

- Confirm safe access with the customer.
- Verify that weather conditions allow safe access.
- Determine whether multiple technicians are required.
- Communicate any known hazards to the assigned technician.
- Support a technician's decision to exercise Stop Work Authority.

---

# Customer Responsibilities

Customers are responsible for providing a safe working environment.

This includes:

- Safe access to equipment.
- Removal of snow, ice, or other access hazards.
- Compliance with required safety procedures.
- Cooperation with Lock Out / Tag Out (LOTO) requirements when applicable.

---

# If Work Is Stopped

If a technician exercises Stop Work Authority:

1. Stop work immediately.
2. Notify the Service Coordinator.
3. Document the reason for stopping work.
4. Inform the customer of the identified safety concern.
5. Resume work only after the hazard has been eliminated or adequately controlled.

---

# Common Stop Work Examples

- Rodent infestation
- Unsafe ladder
- Missing fall protection
- Snow or ice blocking access
- Customer refuses LOTO
- Technician working alone on a task requiring two people

---

# Guiding Principle

Safety always takes priority over productivity, customer requests, and scheduling.

No employee will be expected to continue working in conditions they reasonably believe to be unsafe.
